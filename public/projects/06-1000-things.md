# 1,000 things
_building a cross-platform icon system_

I built and led the icon system at cash app — and eventually across all of Block — creating the glyphs and establishing the pipeline for 1,000+ icons across every brand, every platform. 1,000 things you shouldn't really notice.

## the audit

I was two years into my time as a product designer at cash app when we hired a design systems lead. I pitched an icon and asset audit noting the gaps, the redundancies, and the plan, and became a founding member of our Systems & Standards team.

our design system, arcade, has reached 95% mobile adoption, with web closely following


## the process

a common misconception is that all iconographers are illustrators making tiny spots to fulfil a request for x number of list-of-nouns. but icons and type work hand in hand to establish the foundational decisions in all components. they need to be flexible



not unlike type, icons have a few shapes and specs where you start in order to establsih a consistent style

Service icons aren't illustrations — they're closer to logos. Customers build trust and muscle memory around them. Adding one means asking millions of people to learn something new. That bar should be high.

## the red team

My job was to hold that bar. Does this feature need its own icon, or is it another flavor of moving money that belongs under an existing glyph? What does this represent to the customer, not the team that built it? What's the actual value?

I poked holes. If the rationale held, I designed the icon. If it didn't, the icon didn't get made. Both outcomes are the job.

## The cascade

One icon is never one icon. Every addition or update pulls a thread through spacing, padding, corner radii, line height, dividers, headers, navigation, and every component they touch — across iOS, Android, and web.

This is why icon work is systems work. Not because icons live in a design system. Because every glyph decision is an infrastructure decision.
